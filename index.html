Who we are

About Boltic

We're a community team based in Buffalo Grove, Illinois.

Boltic is a FIRST Tech Challenge team that designs, builds, and programs a competition robot from scratch every season. We do our own CAD, machining, wiring, and code, and we document the whole process the way real engineers do.

Our first season was Center Stage in 2023-24. Beyond the robot, we run on gracious professionalism: we help other teams, share what we learn, and bring more students into STEM through outreach.

What is FTC?

FIRST Tech Challenge puts students in grades 7–12 on teams that design, build, and program a robot to compete in a new game each year, while learning engineering, teamwork, and gracious professionalism.

Meet the Team

Every robot is the work of several subteams pulling in the same direction.

Team Manager

Coordinates team operations, schedules, and overall project management.

Designs, machines, and assembles the robot's structure and mechanisms.

CAD & Design

Models every part in CAD so the build team can fabricate with precision.

Outreach

Runs demos, mentors younger teams, and grows STEM in the community.

Finance & Documentation

Handles sponsorship, the engineering notebook, and our online presence.

Drive Team

Operates the robot in matches and calls strategy on the field.

Impact

Robots are only half of what we do. We work to grow STEM in our community and help the teams coming up behind us.

Through the program, participating teams will receive:

  • One virtual mentoring session (approximately 65 minutes) every other week (specific plan will be discussed with individual teams)
  • Guidance on robot design, programming, strategy, documentation, outreach, and competition preparation
  • Direct access to experienced FTC students for questions and troubleshooting
  • 1-2 in-person workshops focused on hardware, building techniques, and hands-on engineering skills

The program start date is currently scheduled for July 8st but we will confirm that once we get your contract! We understand that every team has different needs, so sessions can be tailored to the areas where your students would benefit most. Our creations

Our Robot

Explore our robots from each competition season. See how our designs evolved and learn about the engineering behind each iteration.

Meet 1

Our first robot focused on testing core mechanisms but revealed several performance issues. The shooter used a polycarbonate frame with incorrect belt sizing, high friction, and no angle adjustment, making it difficult to align and shoot consistently. The intake relied on surgical tubing flaps that were slow and lacked enough grip and compression, while the storage system used an inline push-arm design that resulted in slow transfers and no game-piece sorting capability.

Meet 2

For our second meet, we strengthened the robot by switching to a fully metal shooter frame, correcting belt sizes, and reducing friction to improve reliability. The intake was redesigned with a compliant arm and grip wheels, which improved game-piece pickup, although the mechanism still had too many moving parts and inconsistent contact due to a steep ramp. We also tested a full-size transfer dish, which improved handling but required a steeper transfer path and was too large for the robot's constraints.

Meet 3

Before the third meet, we focused on refining and simplifying the robot. We added an adjustable hood to the shooter for better shot tuning and continued reducing friction to improve consistency. The intake was simplified by removing unnecessary moving parts, creating more reliable and repeatable game-piece pickup. The transfer system was also redesigned with slimmer transfer arms and a more efficient layout, improving compatibility with the rest of the robot.

League Tournament

By the league meet, the robot had evolved into a reliable competition-ready design. The shooter featured an adjustable hood, tracking turret, fully metal construction, and optimized belt sizing for accurate and consistent scoring. The intake used a fast, curved ramp with compliant wheels to provide smooth and dependable game-piece collection. Finally, the storage and transfer system incorporated a cut-off dish, less steep transfer ramp, and slimmer transfer arms, resulting in faster, more reliable transfers and improved overall scoring efficiency.
